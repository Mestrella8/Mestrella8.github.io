Sewing & Mending

I enjoy thrifting and visiting flea markets, always on the lookout for unique, rare, and second-hand finds that aren’t available in regular stores. Sometimes, I come across items that need a bit of repair, which has led me to try mending, embroidery, and sewing. I believe that buying second-hand is a great way to reduce waste and give clothes a second life. I’ve also experimented with making things from scratch, like tote bags from denim scraps or stretchy velvet yoga pants.

I’ve also volunteered with FABSCRAP, an organization that meets New York City’s commercial textile recycling needs. FABSCRAP prevents materials from ending up in landfills by making them available for reuse. It’s a convenient service that provides affordable and accessible materials, including resellable clothes and fabric for designers and makers. As a volunteer, I’ve had the chance to pick out 5 pounds of free fabric from their collection, which includes damaged clothes from brands like Michael Kors, Coach, and Peloton.

New York City residents throw out 200,000 tons of clothing, shoes, accessories, and linens every year. Textiles make up 6% of the City’s total waste stream. Residential waste—items used and discarded by individuals or families—is picked up by the Department of Sanitation. Commercial waste - what's discarded by a business - is picked up by private carters. The best estimate is that it is at least 40x residential waste. If you’re interested in learning more about fashion waste, I wrote a research paper on this topic in High School titled "The Fast Fashion Industry and its Environmental Impacts".

Ceramics
